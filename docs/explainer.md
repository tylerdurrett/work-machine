# Work Machine

**Let your agents focus on the work, not the workflow.**

## The problem

AI agents are remarkably good at real creative work. You can hand one a task and watch it write, design, draft, and revise. For small jobs, it feels like magic.

Then the job gets big.

Take making a video. A single video can take days, weeks, even months. It moves through a brief, a script, a storyboard, a rough cut, a polish pass, and a string of approvals along the way. It needs structure. It needs a workflow.

So you start teaching your agent the workflow. Do the brief, then wait for approval, then write the script, then post it here, then notify this person, then file the result there. And little by little, something goes wrong.

Your agent is no longer just *doing the creative work*. It's also keeping track of where it is in a ten-step process, remembering who needs to approve what, deciding where files should go, and trying not to skip a step. The creative task — the part you actually care about — is now sharing headspace with a pile of bookkeeping.

The results suffer. The agent makes worse creative calls because it's distracted by mechanics. It makes mechanical mistakes because it's distracted by the creative work. You reach for bigger, slower, more expensive models just to push through the bloated steps. File management gets messy. Getting your teammates to review things is awkward. And the moment you have *several* videos in flight at once, the whole thing becomes nearly impossible to keep straight.

## The idea

Work Machine is built on one simple move: **separate the doing from the managing.**

The *managing* — what happens next, who approves it, where the files live, making sure nothing gets skipped — gets pulled out into a dependable engine that handles it the same way every time. The *doing* gets handed to an agent (or a person, or a script) with a single, clean, focused task and none of the baggage.

We call it a **thick engine and a thin skill.** The engine is heavy and reliable and boring on purpose. The skill is light. Each agent shows up, does one well-defined thing it's genuinely good at, and hands the result back. The right agent works on the right task at the right time — and never has to think about the workflow at all.

That one change ripples outward into everything below.

## What it feels like

Imagine your team has a standard commercial-video workflow. Here's a run from start to finish.

**You kick it off.** You tell your agent you're making a new video. It spins up a fresh run of the team's video workflow and creates a card in Trello — your team's normal issue tracker. You open the card and there's a clear description of the video to come, a diagram of the whole workflow ahead, and a marker showing exactly where things stand right now.

**The brief writes itself.** The engine sees the new run and gets to work. Using your description and the brand materials, an agent drafts a full creative brief, then files it as its own card for review. The right teammates get a Slack ping: *a brief is ready.* The owner clicks through to Trello, reads it, and leaves a comment with feedback. The agent reads the comment, revises the brief, and reposts. Back and forth until it's approved — and the entire review is right there on the record, start to finish.

**The script comes next.** Same rhythm. This time the reviewer is out on a walk, so he replies straight from his chat assistant, which updates the card for him. He never even opens Trello. The decision still lands on the card, documented like all the others.

**Then storyboards.** The agent generates a range of sample frames for each scene — sometimes many variations to choose from. Each choice becomes its own little card, so every decision and review is captured.

**Then a rough cut — no AI needed.** A plain script stitches the storyboard frames together with the voiceover and posts it to Frame.io, where your team already knows how to leave timestamped comments. When the notes are in, they're gathered back onto the main card and the agent tightens up the timing.

**Then polish — done by a human.** This step is just a person doing the work. And nothing about the system changes. Same workflow, same cards, same review process. Human work slots right in next to the agent work, no special handling.

**Then it wraps up.** When the final cut is approved, an agent writes a short summary of the work and drafts a client email for review.

And here's the part that's easy to miss: you can have many of these running at once. Several videos, all in flight, with agents quietly picking up whatever step is ready on whichever project. You're not babysitting any of it.

## What you get

- **More reliable work.** Each agent gets a small, focused task instead of a bloated one, so it makes better creative calls — and the engine guarantees the steps themselves happen correctly, in order, every time.
- **Your team can actually see it.** Work lives in the tools you already use — Trello, Slack, Frame.io — so teammates can follow along, review, and contribute without learning anything new.
- **A map, and a "you are here."** Because the workflow is defined up front, you can see the whole process *and* where any given project stands right now. No more squinting at a pile of hand-stitched steps trying to figure out what's left.
- **People, agents, and scripts are interchangeable.** Any step can be done by an AI agent, a plain script, or a human — and the rest of the workflow doesn't notice the difference.
- **Every decision is on the record.** Approvals, feedback, and revisions are all captured right in the tracker, so there's never a question about what happened or who signed off.
- **Many jobs at once, and much bigger ones.** With the mechanics handled by the engine, you can run lots of projects in parallel and take on far longer, larger jobs than you'd ever attempt by hand.
